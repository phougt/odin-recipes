Nombanhjok

Desciption

Nom banh chok is a beloved Cambodian dish, so much so that in English it’s sometimes called simply “Khmer noodles.” It’s a typical breakfast food, and every morning you’ll find it being sold by women carrying baskets of fresh rice noodles hanging from a pole balanced on their shoulders. The dish consists of fresh noodles laboriously pounded out of rice, topped with a fish-based green curry gravy made from lemongrass, fingerroot ginger, turmeric, and garlic. Fresh cucumbers, banana flower, long beans, edible flowers, and wild leaves are heaped on top. In Siem Reap, it is served with a sweet sauce called tuk paem made from palm sugar and peanuts.

Ingredients

  • 1/2 teaspoon Sugar palm sugar for a caramel flavor
  • 400 ml Coconut milk coconut cream as an alternative
  • 1/2 tablespoon Prahok some iterations of this dish may call for kapi, or shrimp paste
  • 1/2 teaspoon MSG
  • 1/2 teaspoon Salt
  • 3 quarts Cold water
  • 1 inch turmeric root use 1 teaspoon of powdered turmeric as an alternative
  • 3 stalks Lemongrass
  • 5 leaves Kaffir lime leaves
  • 5 Garlic cloves
  • 1 Mudfish (medium sized, approximately 1 pound) any freshwater fish will do
  • 1½ inch Galangal
  • 3 Fingerroot ginger ginger will do as an alternative
  • 2 stalks Green onions
  • 1 lb Rice noodles Bun Tuoi dry rice vermicelli works well

Steps

  1. Scale and gut the mudfish, then boil in a large pot for 20 minutes.
  2. Add a pinch of salt to the fish.
  3. Cut lemongrass into thin slices and place into the mortar.
  4. Clean and thinly cut galangal, turmeric, fingerroot ginger, chilli peppers, and garlic cloves, and place in the mortar.
  5. Slice kaffir lime leaves into strips and add to the mortar.
  6. Pound the ingredients in the mortar using a pestle until you achieve a paste-like consistency.
  7. Remove the fish from the boiling water and set aside.
  8. Strain the water in which you boiled the fish through a sieve and set the fish broth aside.
  9. Separate the fish meat from the skin and skeleton, ensuring that you discard all bones. Add the fish meat to the mortar with the kreung paste, but discard the skeleton, skin, and head.
  10. Gently pound the fish meat together with the kreung paste until you create an even mixture of all ingredients.
  11. Boil half a can of the coconut milk in a large pot over medium heat.
  12. Add the fish mixture to the boiling coconut milk and mix together.
  13. Place the fish broth into the pot and mix all the ingredients together.
  14. Add prahok to the pot and stir.
  15. Add the other half of the coconut milk can to the pot and mix all the ingredients together.
  16. Add all the remaining ingredients to the pot and stir.
  17. Cook the resulting fish curry for 15 minutes over medium heat.
  18. Cut green onions and add them to the fish curry just before turning the heat off.
  19. Remove the fish curry from the heat.
  20. Boil the rice noodles until soft (usually 10 minutes), then remove from heat and strain.
  21. Place julienned cucumbers, along with herbs and greens of your choice, into large serving bowls (1 per diner).
  22. Distribute the boiled rice noodles among the diners' bowls, placing them on top of the vegetables.
  23. Pour the fish curry over top of the rice noodles.
  24. Sprinkle the sesbania flowers over top of the fish curry.
